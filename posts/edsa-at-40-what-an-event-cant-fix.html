EDSA at 40: What an Event Can't Fix

A four-part reflection

About the Author

Richard Cruz is a strategic consultant and educator who works with organizations navigating complex transformation in business, governance, and institutional design. He is the founder of Shift Consulting and an Adjunct Professor at the Asian Institute of Management, where he spent twenty-five years teaching finance, strategy, and innovation. His interests include systems, institutions, organizational learning, and the gap between what organizations intend and what they actually build.

This essay reflects his own thinking and no institutional position.

Part One: Why I'm Reflecting on EDSA This Way

Forty years is a useful distance.

Far enough to see the shape of things — what held, what didn't, what we told ourselves versus what the evidence eventually showed. Close enough that many of the people who lived it are still here, still thinking, still capable of being honest about what they expected and what actually happened.

I want to use that distance carefully.

This essay is not a commemorative piece. I have enormous respect for what happened in February 1986 — the courage it required, the improbability of it, the genuine moral clarity of the moment. But commemoration is not the same as reflection, and reflection is what forty years actually earns us. Commemoration tends to freeze meaning. Reflection allows meaning to develop.

This is also not a political positioning piece. I am not interested in relitigating who was right, who was wrong, or which faction of the post-EDSA period best honored its promise. That argument has been running for four decades and has generated more heat than light. I am deliberately stepping outside of it.

What I am interested in is the structural question underneath the political one: What does it actually take for a political reset to become a durable transformation?

That question matters because EDSA was not unique in kind. History is full of moments when citizens collectively altered the direction of their country, when enough people decided that the existing arrangement was no longer acceptable and acted on that decision. Some of those moments produced lasting change. Many did not. The difference was rarely about the quality of the moment itself. It was about what was built afterward, and whether the conditions for building it existed in the first place.

I tend to think in terms of systems and institutional design. That is not a neutral lens, no lens is, but it is a transparent one. I care more about mechanisms than about personalities, more about the architecture of institutions than about the intentions of the people inside them. Good people inside badly designed systems tend to produce bad outcomes. Mediocre people inside well-designed systems can produce surprisingly good ones. This is not a cynical observation. It is, I think, an empirical one.

Events can reset leadership. Systems determine long-term outcomes.

EDSA reset leadership. What it could not do — what no single event can do — is redesign the systems through which leadership operates. That work is slower, less dramatic, less visible. It does not produce the kind of solidarity that fills streets. But it is the work that determines whether a reset holds.

This essay explores that distinction in four parts. The first — this one — is about the frame I am using and why. The second examines EDSA through a structural lens: what it was, what it could not be, and what the gap between the two reveals. The third looks at the preconditions for durable transformation — the cognitive, institutional, economic, and cultural conditions that make a reset stick. The fourth turns toward possibility: not grand reform, but small, well-designed experiments that could compound over time.

I am writing for people who are already asking structural questions — who have moved past the anniversary-as-nostalgia reflex and want a frame for thinking about where we actually are and what, if anything, is worth doing.

If you are looking for affirmation of a position you already hold, this will probably frustrate you. That is intentional.

Part Two: Processing EDSA at 40 — Event vs. System

Start with what it was.

EDSA was a moment of collective civic agency at a scale the Philippines had not previously demonstrated. For four days in February 1986, hundreds of thousands of ordinary people — not soldiers, not party operatives, not a single commanding organization — placed themselves between a failing dictatorship and the military units that had defected from it. They did so without a script, largely without violence, and with a clarity of intent that surprised even those who had organized the initial call. The dictatorship collapsed. Power transferred. The world watched.

That matters. It should not be minimized or subjected to retroactive cynicism. The people on those streets were acting on a genuine moral judgment — that what had been done to their country for two decades was wrong, that it had to stop, and that they were willing to bear personal risk to say so. Whatever structural critique follows, that fact deserves to stand on its own.

But here is where the anniversary invites honesty.

EDSA was a political reset. It removed a leadership configuration that had become predatory and replaced it with one that was more legitimate and more humane. That is not a small thing. Resets of that kind are genuinely rare, and they matter. But a reset is not a redesign. Removing a bad driver does not repair the road, retrain the passengers, or change the destination on the map. It changes who is steering.

What EDSA could not do, by its nature, was redesign the incentive structures that had allowed the dictatorship to consolidate power in the first place. Those structures — the concentration of economic assets in a small number of families, the weakness of horizontal accountability mechanisms, the personalistic logic of political loyalty, the dependence of local governance on vertical patronage networks — were older than Marcos and survived him. They were the water the Philippine political economy swam in.

EDSA changed the fish at the top of the tank. It did not change the water.

This is not a pessimistic reading. It is a precise one.

The distinction between mobilization and institutionalization is not a distinction between hope and despair. It is a distinction between two kinds of work, operating on different timescales and requiring different skill sets.

Mobilization is episodic. It concentrates energy, breaks through blockages, and makes visible what had been normalized. Think of it as clearing a blocked drain — necessary, dramatic, immediately satisfying.

Institutionalization is continuous. It is the slower work of redesigning the plumbing so the drain does not block again — building the rules, norms, feedback loops, and accountability mechanisms that make good outcomes repeatable rather than heroic.

Durable democratic transformation requires both. But they do not happen simultaneously, and they cannot be produced by the same event. The error — repeated not only in the Philippines but in transitions around the world — is to treat mobilization as transformation, and then to experience the gap between what was expected and what followed as betrayal rather than as a structural gap.

The gap is structural. That framing matters.

It means the problem is not primarily that the wrong people took over after EDSA. It means that no single event — however morally significant, however broadly supported — can produce the institutional depth that durable democracy requires. That depth has to be built deliberately, unglamorously, over decades, and against the resistance of those whose interests are served by keeping institutions weak.

Forty years later, that work is still underway. In some areas, it has advanced. In others, it has been actively reversed. The question is not whether EDSA succeeded or failed — that framing is too binary and too focused on personalities.

The more useful question is: what is the state of the institutional infrastructure that determines whether political resets of any kind can produce durable improvement?

That is a harder question. It does not yield a clear verdict on EDSA, the administrations that followed, or any particular political actor. It demands structural accounting rather than political accounting.

Which is exactly why most anniversary discourse avoids it.

Part Three: Preconditions for Durable Transformation

If events reset leadership while systems determine outcomes, the question that follows is not philosophical — it is diagnostic: what conditions make it possible to build the systems that durable transformation requires?

Post-transition research across Latin America, Eastern Europe, Southeast Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa has produced some recurring patterns. The picture is not simple. But it is not mysterious either.

Four categories of preconditions recur with enough consistency to take seriously: cognitive, institutional, economic, and cultural. They are not independent — they interact, reinforce, and sometimes undermine each other. But separating them analytically is useful because they operate on different timescales and respond to different kinds of intervention.

Cognitive Preconditions

The first precondition is perhaps the least discussed, because it is the most uncomfortable: the capacity of citizens, leaders, and institutions to think structurally about their own situation.

This is not about intelligence or formal education. It is about a specific skill — the ability to distinguish events from mechanisms, to ask not just what happened but why the conditions existed for it to happen, and to resist the pull toward explanation by personality when the real explanation lies in structure.

It is also about tolerance for delayed gratification: the willingness to pursue reforms whose benefits are diffuse and measured in decades, not election cycles.

This skill is not automatically produced by democratic experience. In fact, competitive democratic politics can actively suppress it — because structural explanations are harder to communicate than personal ones. The politician who says the system is broken and that there is a 15-year plan to fix it consistently loses to the one who says, "I alone can fix it." That asymmetry is not a Philippine problem. It is a human one.

What matters is whether enough people — in civic institutions, academia, business, and communities — are thinking structurally and producing analysis that can gradually shift the cognitive baseline. Not loudly. Not immediately. But consistently enough to create an alternative intellectual infrastructure to the one that reduces everything to personalities and betrayals.

Institutional Preconditions

The second category is more familiar. Durable transformation requires institutions capable of generating reliable feedback, enforcing accountability, and resisting capture by the interests they are supposed to regulate.

Three mechanisms matter most.

Horizontal accountability — the capacity of institutions to check each other, independent of electoral cycles. Courts, audit bodies, legislative oversight, and independent regulatory agencies: these are the shock absorbers of democratic systems. When they are weak or captured, a leadership change at the top produces little structural change below.

Process transparency — not just the publication of information, but the design of processes where decisions are visible, rationale is documented, and the logic chain from decision to outcome can be examined and contested. Opacity is not only a corruption problem. It is a learning problem. Systems that cannot be examined cannot be improved.

Incentive alignment — ensuring that the formal rewards and sanctions on institutional actors are consistent with the behaviors the system actually needs. When prosecutors are rewarded for conviction rates rather than justice outcomes, when legislators are rewarded for local pork rather than national policy coherence, the system produces exactly what its incentives demand — regardless of the intentions of the people inside it.

None of these mechanisms builds itself. They require deliberate design, sustained political will to defend them, and enough time to develop organizational cultures that make them self-reinforcing rather than dependent on heroic individuals.

Economic Preconditions

The relationship between economic development and democratic consolidation is well-documented. The precise version that holds across the evidence is this: broad-based economic participation stabilizes democratic systems, while extreme economic power concentration destabilizes them.

The mechanism is straightforward. When economic assets are concentrated in a small number of families or networks, those families have both the motive and the means to shape political outcomes in their favor — through campaign financing, media ownership, regulatory capture, and the simple fact that political careers depend on their cooperation. This does not require a conspiracy. It is the normal operation of self-interest where rules are weak enough to bend.

Conversely, when economic opportunity is broadly distributed — when a significant middle class exists, when regional economies are resilient, when small and medium enterprises constitute a meaningful counterweight to conglomerate concentration — a larger proportion of the population has a rational interest in rule-based competition rather than relationship-based access. Democracy becomes, in economic terms, the rational choice for more people.

This is why regional economic development is not just an equity issue. It is a governance issue. A country whose economic activity is concentrated in one metropolitan area is also a country whose political gravity is concentrated in one place — creating structural pressure toward centralization, personalization, and winner-take-all politics.

Cultural Preconditions

Culture is both real and manipulable — it can describe genuine behavioral patterns or be weaponized to explain away structural problems that are actually amenable to design solutions. That caveat noted, culture matters.

What matters is not culture in the broad, essentialist sense — not claims about Filipino values or Asian political culture or frameworks historically used to argue that certain populations are less suited to democratic governance. That argument has a weak empirical foundation and a troubling political history. What matters are specific norms and their institutional expression.

Three norms are particularly consequential.

Procedural fairness — the widespread expectation that rules apply equally, regardless of relationship, status, or wealth. Where this norm is strong, institutions tend toward consistency. Where it is weak, institutions become arenas for negotiating exceptions, and rule-following becomes the behavior only of those without the connections to do otherwise.

Trust calibration — specifically, the gradual transfer of social trust from individuals to institutions. In post-authoritarian societies, trust tends to be intensely personalistic: people trust specific individuals rather than offices or processes. This is not irrational — institutions have often failed, and personal relationships have often been the only reliable navigation mechanism. But it creates a ceiling. When trust cannot transfer to institutions, institutions cannot develop the authority they need to function independently of whoever happens to occupy them at any given moment.

Patience for incremental reform — the cultural tolerance for improvement that is real but slow, unglamorous, and resistant to a clear narrative. This is perhaps the hardest precondition to build, because it runs against the grain of democratic politics, which rewards the dramatic over the structural. Without it, reform cycles tend to be episodic: bursts of energy following crises, then fatigue, and finally the gradual reassertion of the patterns reform was meant to displace.

What This Tells Us

These four categories are not a checklist. They are a diagnostic frame.

Looking at the Philippines forty years after EDSA, each precondition has developed unevenly — genuine advances in some areas, significant reversals in others, persistent structural gaps that no single administration has systematically addressed.

That unevenness is not a reason for despair. It is a reason for precision. The question is not whether the Philippines is ready for transformation — that framing is too binary and too passive.

The question is: which preconditions are most binding right now, and what kinds of interventions have the best chance of strengthening them?

That question leads directly to the fourth and final part.

Part Four: What Is Actually Worth Doing

There is a particular kind of frustration that serious structural analysis can produce.

You follow the argument. You accept the diagnosis. You recognize the preconditions and can see, with reasonable clarity, how far the current reality falls short of them. And then the natural question arises: so what do we do? And if the answer is — build cognitive capacity, reform incentive structures, develop regional economies, shift cultural norms — the response is understandable: that is everything, it will take generations, and I am one person with limited time and limited leverage.

That frustration is legitimate. But it rests on an assumption worth examining: that meaningful action requires comprehensive reform, and that anything short of that is either marginal or complicit in the status quo.

That assumption is wrong.

Why Grand Reform Usually Fails

Large-scale institutional redesign fails more often than it succeeds — not primarily because of bad intentions or insufficient political will, though both can contribute. It fails because complex systems do not respond predictably to large interventions. The system has more variables than any design process can account for, more actors with more interests than any coalition can fully align, and more unintended consequences than any implementation team can anticipate.

This is not an argument for passivity. It is an argument for a different theory of change.

The history of durable institutional improvement — in governance, public health, education, and organizational development — reveals a consistent pattern: transformation tends to emerge from the accumulation of small, well-designed experiments rather than from single large redesign attempts. Not because small is inherently better, but because small experiments can fail safely, generate real feedback, be modified before scaling, and build the local knowledge that larger reforms require but rarely take the time to develop.

The question then becomes not what the comprehensive solution is, but what experiments are worth running.

What Makes an Experiment Worth Running

A well-designed probe has three characteristics that distinguish it from either a pilot program or a symbolic gesture.

Reversibility. The experiment can be stopped or modified without locking in consequences worse than the problem it was trying to address. Many reform initiatives violate this — they create new bureaucratic structures or political expectations that make them harder to stop than to continue, even when the evidence suggests they are not working.

Signal clarity. The experiment is designed with specific, observable indicators that can tell you, within a reasonable timeframe, whether it is producing the intended effect. Without this, experiments become articles of faith — defended by their designers and attacked by their opponents, with no shared evidentiary basis for honest evaluation.

Learning orientation. The primary purpose is not to demonstrate success but to generate knowledge — about what works, under what conditions, for whom, and at what cost. This requires a genuine willingness to document and share negative results, which runs against the grain of most institutional cultures where failure is career risk rather than organizational learning.

When these three characteristics are present, small experiments can do something that comprehensive reform plans rarely can: they can build genuine confidence — not the confidence of conviction, but the confidence of evidence.

What This Could Look Like

The following are not prescriptions. They are illustrations of the kind of intervention that could, at a modest scale, begin to strengthen one or more of the preconditions from Part Three. Each is small enough to be initiated by a single committed institution or network, without requiring national political alignment. Each is observable enough to generate feedback. And each, if it works, has a plausible path to broader adoption.

Local governance transparency dashboards. Municipal or barangay-level platforms that make budget allocations, procurement decisions, and project status visible to constituents — designed not as compliance tools but as genuine feedback mechanisms. The question being tested: Does process transparency at the most immediate scale of governance change the behavior of local officials and the expectations of local constituents?

A starting move: Publish a monthly one-page procurement and project status digest for three pilot barangays and track whether question volume from constituents changes within 90 days.

Policy stress-testing forums in universities. Structured deliberative processes, hosted by academic institutions, in which proposed legislation is subjected to scenario analysis, second-order effects mapping, and stakeholder impact assessment before adoption. Universities are among the few institutions with enough cross-partisan credibility to host this without immediate capture by political framing.

A starting move: Run one forum per quarter on a single active bill, publish the scenario map and second-order effects analysis, and make it freely available to legislators and media.

Citizen budget literacy programs. Not financial education in the conventional sense, but structured experiences that allow ordinary citizens to engage with actual budget trade-offs at the local level — to understand what a municipal budget contains, what discretionary decisions it encodes, and what alternatives were available. The question being tested: does direct engagement with budget reality produce the kind of informed civic expectation that generates accountability pressure from below?

A starting move: A 90-minute workshop using the actual local budget and three forced trade-offs, run with a community organization already embedded in the barangay.

Regional SME governance and innovation pilots. Clusters of small and medium enterprises in secondary cities, supported by structured peer learning and governance capacity building, are designed to test whether regional economic actors can develop the institutional sophistication to engage with local government on terms that are not purely transactional.

A starting move: A 12-week peer circle with one shared governance metric and one joint LGU engagement, designed to produce a single documented case — positive or negative — that others can learn from.

The specific examples matter less than the logic behind them. Identify the binding precondition. Design a small experiment that tests a specific mechanism for strengthening it. Build in signal clarity and learning orientation from the start. Create conditions for honest evaluation.

The Closing Question

Forty years is long enough to stop waiting for the right leader.

That is not a cynical statement. It is a structural one. The evidence from four decades of post-EDSA Philippine political history — and from comparable transitions elsewhere — is consistent: leadership quality matters, but it operates within institutional constraints; it rarely has the capacity, and almost never has the incentive, to fundamentally redesign.

The leader who arrives committed to institutional reform faces a system whose existing beneficiaries are organized against that reform, whose incentive structures reward personal loyalty over procedural integrity, and whose political survival depends on the same patronage networks that reform would disrupt. Good leaders navigate this with more integrity than bad ones. But they navigate it — they do not escape it.

What escapes it, slowly, is institutional infrastructure. Rules that become self-enforcing. Norms that become expectations. Feedback mechanisms that become habits. Accountability structures that develop their own constituencies. These do not emerge from heroic leadership. They emerge from the patient accumulation of small, well-designed improvements — defended by the coalitions they create and gradually embedded in the culture they shape.

The question worth asking on this anniversary is not whether EDSA succeeded or failed, or who honored its promise and who betrayed it. Those questions have been asked for forty years and have produced forty years of circular argument.

The question worth asking is this: what small structural upgrades, initiated now and compounding over the next twenty years, could produce the institutional depth that EDSA's moment required but could not create?

And beneath that, the more personal question each reader has to answer: in the decisions you make, the organizations you lead, the institutions you participate in, the standards you hold, and the ones you quietly let slide —

Are you reinforcing personalities or institutions?

That is not a question about EDSA.

It is a question about today.

A Note on Process and Authorship

This essay began with a question I have been carrying for some time: what do people who are serious about the Philippines actually need from an EDSA anniversary reflection? Not commemoration, not political verdict, but a framework for understanding and for action.

The four-part structure came from that question. I wanted to reach a particular kind of reader, someone already looking for a structural frame rather than a commemorative one, and move them from diagnosis to preconditions to possibility. The core intellectual commitment driving the essay is one I work with daily across sectors: that you cannot design transformation from the top down in complex systems. You create the enabling conditions, and you run well-designed experiments. The essay, in that sense, is an application of a professional framework to a historical moment I have lived through.

What I did not do alone was produce the prose. I worked with an AI system in an extended structured dialogue. I provided the outline, the intended audience, the core arguments, and the directional decisions throughout. The AI developed the language, proposed structural options, and drafted each section. I redirected the tone when it drifted toward the purely commemorative, pushed for the preconditions framework as the intellectual core, and made specific editorial decisions at every stage, including which reviewer suggestions to accept and which to reject.

The arguments in this essay are ones I can defend, extend, and be challenged on in any conversation. The ideas are part of the work I do. The prose was developed collaboratively with a tool best understood as an unusually capable thinking partner, one that requires you to know what you want to get something worth reading.

I am disclosing this not as a disclaimer but as a position. The question of how AI-assisted intellectual work should be attributed is one that serious people are navigating across academia, journalism, and professional practice right now. My view is that transparency about process is a condition of intellectual integrity, and that the distinction that matters is not whether AI touched the prose, but whether the human author is accountable for every argument.

I am.
